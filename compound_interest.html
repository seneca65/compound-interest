What is compound interest?
Compound interest means you earn interest not just on your original money, but also on the interest you've already earned. Over time this creates a snowball effect — your money grows faster and faster the longer it sits.
Example: $1,000 at 7% earns $70 in year one. In year two you earn 7% on $1,070 — that's $74.90. Each year the interest amount grows, even without adding new money.
The Rule of 72
Divide 72 by your annual interest rate to find roughly how many years it takes your money to double.
At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
At 8%: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double
At 10%: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double

This means money invested at 10% doubles roughly every 7 years — and doubles again 7 years after that.
Where does the 7% come from?
The S&P 500 — an index of the 500 largest US companies — has returned an average of roughly 10% per year since 1926. After subtracting the long-run US inflation rate of about 3%, the "real" return is approximately 7%. This is why financial planners often use 7% as a conservative baseline for long-term projections. Past returns don't guarantee future results, but nearly a century of data makes it a reasonable estimate.
What is an index fund?
An index fund simply tracks a market index — like the S&P 500 — rather than having a manager pick individual stocks. Because no one is actively trading, costs stay extremely low. The expense ratio (annual fee) on funds like VTI or VOO is just 0.03% — that's $3 per year on every $10,000 invested.
A fund charging 1% vs 0.03% on a $300,000 portfolio costs you roughly $2,910 more per year — money that would otherwise be compounding for you.
The power of starting early
Time is the most powerful variable in compound interest — more powerful than rate or how much you save per week.
Saving $50/week at 7%:

Start at age 25 → $570,000 at age 70
Start at age 35 → $272,000 at age 70
Start at age 45 → $117,000 at age 70

Starting 10 years earlier more than doubles the outcome. Starting 20 years earlier multiplies it nearly 5 times.
Total Expense Ratio (TER) — what to look for
The Total Expense Ratio (TER) is the annual fee a fund charges, expressed as a percentage of your investment. It is deducted automatically — you never write a check, but it quietly reduces your returns every year.
What to look for:
Under 0.10% — excellent (index ETFs like VTI, VOO)
0.10% – 0.50% — acceptable for specialty funds
Over 0.50% — high; look for a cheaper alternative
Over 1.00% — very high; hard to justify long term

Why it matters over time:
$100,000 invested for 30 years at 7%:
At 0.03% TER → ~$760,000
At 1.00% TER → ~$574,000
That difference costs you roughly $186,000.

Other things to check when picking an ETF or fund:

• Expense ratio — as low as possible
• Index it tracks — broad market (S&P 500, total market) is simpler and historically hard to beat
• Fund size — larger funds (over $1 billion) are more stable and liquid
• Issuer reputation — Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab are well established
• Actively managed vs. passive — passive index funds consistently outperform most active managers over long periods, at a fraction of the cost
How this calculator works
Your weekly savings amount is converted to a monthly deposit. Each month the calculator applies the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12) to your current balance and adds your deposit. This is the standard Future Value of Annuity formula used by financial calculators. The inflation adjustment divides the final balance by (1 + inflation rate) raised to the power of the number of years — showing what that future money is worth in today's dollars.
